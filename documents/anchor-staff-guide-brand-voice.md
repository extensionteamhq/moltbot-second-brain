# How to Create a Brand Voice Style Guide
## A Complete Framework for Defining How Your Brand Communicates

> **Related Tool:** Use the [Brand Voice Intake Form](/?doc=anchor-staff-brand-voice-intake-form) to collect this information from clients systematically.

### What Is a Brand Voice Style Guide?

A Brand Voice Style Guide is a document that defines how your brand speaks, writes, and communicates with the world. It ensures consistency across every touchpoint—whether it's a social media post, an email, a sales conversation, or a customer service interaction.

**Why It Matters:**
- Customers recognize and trust consistent voices
- Team members and contractors can communicate on-brand without constant oversight
- Your messaging becomes distinctive in a crowded market
- It scales your brand beyond just you

**Who Uses This Guide:**
- Marketing team members
- Content creators and copywriters
- Social media managers
- Sales team
- Customer service representatives
- External contractors and agencies

## Section 1: Brand Personality

### What to Include

Define your brand as if it were a person. This helps everyone understand the "character" behind the communication.

### 1.1 Brand Personality Traits

**What It Is:** 3-5 adjectives that describe your brand's character.

**How to Define It:**
Ask yourself: If your brand walked into a room, how would people describe them?

**Deliverable Format:**

| Trait | What It Means | What It Doesn't Mean |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| Confident | We speak with authority and conviction | Not arrogant or dismissive |
| Approachable | We're friendly and easy to talk to | Not unprofessional or too casual |
| Knowledgeable | We demonstrate expertise | Not condescending or jargon-heavy |
| Honest | We tell the truth, even when hard | Not blunt or hurtful |
| Energetic | We bring enthusiasm and momentum | Not manic or exhausting |

**Example:**
> "Anchor & Staff is **confident** (we know our stuff and aren't afraid to say it), **approachable** (we make complex topics simple and inviting), and **honest** (we tell clients what they need to hear, not what they want to hear)."

### 1.2 Brand Archetype

**What It Is:** A universal character type that represents your brand's role in the customer's journey.

**Common Archetypes:**
- **The Sage** — Wisdom, knowledge, expertise (consultants, educators)
- **The Hero** — Courage, achievement, transformation (fitness, coaching)
- **The Caregiver** — Nurturing, service, protection (healthcare, nonprofits)
- **The Creator** — Innovation, imagination, vision (design, technology)
- **The Ruler** — Leadership, control, premium quality (luxury, enterprise)
- **The Everyman** — Relatability, belonging, authenticity (community brands)
- **The Rebel** — Disruption, change, revolution (challenger brands)
- **The Magician** — Transformation, making dreams real (lifestyle brands)

**Deliverable Format:**
> "Our brand archetype is **The Sage**. We position ourselves as the wise guide who has been where our customers want to go. We lead with knowledge and expertise, helping our audience navigate complex decisions with confidence."

### 1.3 Brand Character Statement

**What It Is:** A short paragraph describing your brand's personality as if it were a person.

**Deliverable Format:**
> "If [Brand Name] were a person, they would be [description]. They would [how they interact]. When you talk to them, you feel [emotional response]. They never [what they avoid], and they always [what they do consistently]."

**Example:**
> "If Anchor & Staff were a person, they would be a successful entrepreneur who's 'been there, done that' but hasn't forgotten what it's like to struggle. They'd be the friend who gives you straight talk over coffee—no sugarcoating, but also no judgment. When you talk to them, you feel understood and equipped. They never make you feel stupid for not knowing something, and they always leave you with a clear next step."

## Section 2: Voice Attributes

### What to Include

Break down how your personality traits translate into actual communication patterns.

### 2.1 Voice Attribute Spectrum

**What It Is:** Where your brand falls on key communication spectrums.

**Deliverable Format:**

| Spectrum | Our Position | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Formal ←→ Casual | 40% formal | Professional but not stiff; we use contractions and conversational language |
| Serious ←→ Playful | 70% serious | We're focused on results, but we're not afraid to use humor occasionally |
| Reserved ←→ Enthusiastic | 60% enthusiastic | We're energized about helping clients, without being over-the-top |
| Technical ←→ Simple | 30% technical | We explain complex concepts simply; jargon only when necessary |
| Traditional ←→ Modern | 70% modern | Contemporary language and references; we're current, not dated |

### 2.2 Voice Do's and Don'ts

**What It Is:** Specific guidance on what to do and avoid.

**Deliverable Format:**

**DO:**
- Use "you" and "your" to speak directly to the reader
- Use active voice ("We build systems" not "Systems are built by us")
- Include specific examples and numbers when possible
- Acknowledge challenges honestly before presenting solutions
- End with clear calls to action

**DON'T:**
- Use corporate jargon ("synergy," "leverage," "circle back")
- Talk down to the audience or assume they know nothing
- Make promises we can't keep
- Use excessive exclamation points!!!
- Be negative about competitors (focus on our value instead)

## Section 3: Tone Variations

### What to Include

Your voice stays consistent, but your tone adjusts based on context—just like a real person.

### 3.1 Tone by Context

**What It Is:** How your voice adapts to different situations while maintaining consistency.

**Deliverable Format:**

| Context | Tone Adjustment | Example |
|---------|-----------------|---------|
| **Website homepage** | Confident, inspiring, clear | "Stop guessing. Start generating revenue." |
| **Blog post** | Educational, helpful, thorough | "Let's break down exactly how this works..." |
| **Social media** | Conversational, engaging, concise | "Hot take: Your website has 5 seconds to work. Most fail." |
| **Email newsletter** | Personal, valuable, actionable | "Hey [Name], here's something I've been thinking about..." |
| **Sales conversation** | Consultative, honest, solutions-focused | "Based on what you've shared, here's what I'd recommend..." |
| **Customer support** | Empathetic, helpful, solution-oriented | "I hear you—that's frustrating. Here's how we'll fix it..." |
| **Error/bad news** | Honest, accountable, forward-looking | "We made a mistake. Here's what happened and how we're fixing it." |
| **Celebration/good news** | Enthusiastic, grateful, specific | "This is huge! [Client] just hit $100K in revenue using our system." |

### 3.2 Emotional Range

**What It Is:** The spectrum of emotions your brand expresses and when.

**Deliverable Format:**

| Emotion | When We Express It | How We Express It |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Excitement** | Client wins, new offerings, milestones | "This is what we live for!" (genuine, not performative) |
| **Empathy** | Customer struggles, market challenges | "We get it. This is hard." (acknowledging, not pitying) |
| **Confidence** | Recommendations, expertise areas | "Here's what works." (authoritative, not arrogant) |
| **Urgency** | Time-sensitive offers, important deadlines | "Don't miss this." (clear stakes, not manipulative) |
| **Gratitude** | Customer loyalty, team achievements | "Thank you for trusting us." (specific, not generic) |

## Section 4: Language Guidelines

### What to Include

Specific words, phrases, and writing mechanics that define your brand.

### 4.1 Vocabulary & Terminology

**What It Is:** Words and phrases your brand uses (and avoids).

**Deliverable Format:**

**Words We Use:**
| Instead of... | We say... | Why |
|---------------|-----------|-----|
| Customers | Clients | Implies partnership, not transaction |
| Cheap | Affordable | Value-focused, not bottom-dollar |
| Problems | Challenges | Solvable, not permanent |
| Buy | Invest | Emphasizes ROI and long-term value |
| Contract | Agreement | Less adversarial |

**Words We Avoid:**
| Word/Phrase | Why We Avoid It |
|-------------|-----------------|
| "Synergy" | Overused corporate jargon |
| "Guru/Ninja/Rockstar" | Feels dated and unserious |
| "Guaranteed results" | Overpromises; we can't control all variables |
| "To be honest..." | Implies we're not honest otherwise |
| "Touching base" | Vague and cliché |

### 4.2 Grammar & Mechanics

**What It Is:** Rules for punctuation, capitalization, and formatting.

**Deliverable Format:**

| Element | Our Standard | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Contractions** | Yes, use them | "We're here to help" not "We are here to help" |
| **Oxford comma** | Yes, always | "Strategy, execution, and results" |
| **Capitalization** | Sentence case for headlines | "How to build your brand" not "How To Build Your Brand" |
| **Numbers** | Spell out one-nine, numerals for 10+ | "Three tips" but "15 strategies" |
| **Exclamation points** | Sparingly (max 1 per piece) | Save for genuine excitement |
| **Emoji** | Selective, platform-appropriate | ✓ Social media, ✓ Casual emails, ✗ Formal proposals |
| **Ampersands** | Only in our name (Anchor & Staff) | "Strategy and execution" not "Strategy & execution" |

### 4.3 Naming Conventions

**What It Is:** How to refer to your brand, products, and services.

**Deliverable Format:**

| Element | Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Company name | Anchor & Staff | Anchor and Staff, A&S, Anchor&Staff |
| First reference | Anchor & Staff | The company, The agency |
| Subsequent references | Anchor & Staff, we, our team | They, it |
| Service names | Revenue Roadmap | revenue roadmap, The Revenue Roadmap™ |

## Section 5: Messaging Framework

### What to Include

Core messages that form the foundation of all communication.

### 5.1 Tagline & Positioning Statement

**What It Is:** Your most distilled brand message.

**Deliverable Format:**

**Tagline:**
> "[Short, memorable phrase that captures your value]"
> Example: "Stop Guessing. Start Generating Revenue."

**Positioning Statement:**
> "For [target audience] who [need/challenge], [Brand Name] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]."
> 
> Example: "For small business owners who are struggling to connect marketing to revenue, Anchor & Staff is the strategic partner that builds brand systems that actually convert because we combine proven frameworks with hands-on implementation."

### 5.2 Core Messages

**What It Is:** 3-5 key messages that should appear consistently across communications.

**Deliverable Format:**

| Message | Supporting Points | When to Use |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| "Strategy without execution is hallucination." | We don't just advise—we implement. We're accountable to results. | Differentiating from consultants |
| "Your customer is the hero." | StoryBrand framework. Your marketing should focus on their transformation. | Teaching brand messaging |
| "Systems that work while you sleep." | Automation. Scalable processes. Recurring revenue. | Selling ongoing value |

### 5.3 Elevator Pitches

**What It Is:** Pre-written responses for common questions.

**Deliverable Format:**

**"What does Anchor & Staff do?"**
> "We help business owners stop guessing at marketing and start generating predictable revenue. We do that through brand strategy, messaging, and building systems that bring in leads consistently."

**"Who do you work with?"**
> "Typically small business owners doing at least $500K in revenue who know they need better marketing but don't have the time or expertise to figure it out themselves."

**"How are you different from other agencies?"**
> "Most agencies are either all strategy (consultants who give advice and leave) or all tactics (people posting on social media without a plan). We combine proven strategic frameworks with actual implementation—and we're accountable to revenue, not vanity metrics."

## Section 7: Implementation

### 7.1 How to Use This Guide

**For Team Members:**
- Read the full guide before creating any content
- Reference the voice attributes and do's/don'ts for every piece
- When in doubt, ask: "Would our brand character say it this way?"

**For Contractors:**
- Share this guide before any work begins
- Require first drafts to be reviewed against the checklist
- Provide feedback referencing specific sections

**For Quality Control:**
- Create a checklist from the do's/don'ts
- Review all external communications before publishing
- Update the guide as the brand evolves

### 7.2 Voice Checklist

Use this checklist to review any piece of content:

- [ ] Does this sound like our brand character?
- [ ] Is the tone appropriate for the context?
- [ ] Are we speaking to the customer's needs (not about ourselves)?
- [ ] Is the language simple and jargon-free?
- [ ] Is there a clear next step or takeaway?
- [ ] Does this follow our grammar and mechanics standards?
- [ ] Would we be proud to have this represent our brand?

## Appendix: Brand Voice Development Worksheet

Use these questions to develop your brand voice:

1. List 5 adjectives that describe your brand's personality.
2. Which archetype best represents your brand's role?
3. If your brand were a celebrity, who would it be? Why?
4. What topics does your brand have strong opinions on?
5. What does your brand never joke about?
6. How would your brand handle delivering bad news?
7. What phrases do you hear your best team members using?
8. What do customers say they love about communicating with you?
9. Who are your competitors, and how does your voice differ?
10. What would make someone immediately recognize a message as yours?
