# EMAIL 2: The Hero's Journey
## "The Mindset Shift That Changes Everything"

**Subject Line:** You've been the villain in your own story
**Send:** Day 2

---

Hey {{first_name}},

Quick question: When someone asks what you do, what do you say?

If you're like most business owners, you probably say something like:

- "I'm a landscaper. We do residential and commercial properties."
- "I run an accounting firm. We've been in business 15 years."
- "I own a marketing agency. We help businesses grow."

These answers aren't wrong. But they're missing something crucial.

**They're all about you.**

And here's the uncomfortable truth: your customers don't care about you. At least not yet. What they care about is themselves‚Äîtheir problems, their goals, their fears.

### The Story Framework

In 2017, I discovered a book that completely changed how I think about marketing. It's called "Building a StoryBrand" by Donald Miller, and the core idea is simple:

**Your customer is the hero. You're the guide.**

Think about every great story you've ever watched or read:

- In Star Wars, Luke Skywalker is the hero. Yoda is the guide.
- In The Hunger Games, Katniss is the hero. Haymitch is the guide.
- In The Lord of the Rings, Frodo is the hero. Gandalf is the guide.

The hero has a problem. They meet a guide who's been there before. The guide gives them a plan. The hero takes action and wins.

**Your marketing should work the same way.**

Your customer is Luke‚Äîconfused, struggling, wanting to succeed but not sure how. You're Yoda‚Äîwise, experienced, there to help them win.

### The 7-Part Framework

The StoryBrand framework breaks down into seven elements:

**1. A Character (The Hero)**
Your customer. Not you‚Äîthem. Who are they? What do they want?

**2. Has a Problem**
Every hero faces obstacles. Your customer has three types of problems:
- *External:* The tangible issue (my lawn looks terrible)
- *Internal:* How it makes them feel (I'm embarrassed when neighbors visit)
- *Philosophical:* Why it's just wrong (Homeowners deserve to enjoy their property)

**3. Meets a Guide (You)**
Guides have two qualities: empathy (I understand your struggle) and authority (I know how to fix it).

**4. Who Gives Them a Plan**
A simple, clear path forward. Usually 3 steps. "Here's how we'll solve this together."

**5. And Calls Them to Action**
A clear next step. Not "learn more"‚Äîa direct ask. "Schedule your free consultation."

**6. That Helps Them Avoid Failure**
What's at stake? What happens if they don't act? Paint the picture (without being manipulative).

**7. And Ends in Success**
What does life look like after working with you? The transformation.

### Real Example: Sarah's Landscaping Company

Let's revisit Sarah from yesterday.

**Before (About Her):**
"Sarah's Landscaping has been serving Ohio for 12 years. We provide residential and commercial landscaping services with attention to detail and customer satisfaction."

*Yawn.*

**After (About the Customer):**

"**Tired of being the house with the worst yard on the block?**

We get it. You work hard all week, and the last thing you want to do on Saturday is battle with your lawn. But every time you pull into the driveway, that overgrown mess reminds you of one more thing you're failing at.

**It doesn't have to be this way.**

At Sarah's Landscaping, we give busy homeowners their weekends back with reliable, affordable lawn care that makes the neighbors jealous.

Here's how it works:
1. Tell us about your property (takes 2 minutes)
2. We'll send you a custom quote within 24 hours
3. Sit back and enjoy a lawn you're proud of

**Get your free quote today** and reclaim your weekends."

See the difference? The second version:
- Leads with the customer's problem
- Acknowledges how they feel
- Positions Sarah as the guide
- Gives a simple 3-step plan
- Clear call to action
- Paints the success picture

### Your Action Step

Grab a piece of paper and answer these questions for YOUR business:

1. **Who is your customer?** (Be specific‚Äî"busy homeowners" not "anyone with a lawn")
2. **What's their external problem?** (The tangible thing they're trying to solve)
3. **What's their internal problem?** (How does the external problem make them feel?)
4. **What's the philosophical problem?** (Why is this situation just wrong?)
5. **What plan do you offer?** (3 simple steps)
6. **What does failure look like?** (If they don't act)
7. **What does success look like?** (The transformation)

Write it down. This becomes the DNA of all your marketing‚Äîyour website, your ads, your emails, your sales conversations. Everything.

### The Pro Tip

Most businesses get stuck on the "guide" part. They either:
- Lack empathy (too salesy, don't acknowledge the struggle)
- Lack authority (don't demonstrate they can actually help)

You need both. "I understand where you are, AND I know how to get you where you want to be."

- What does this company do?
- How does it make my life better?
- What am I supposed to do next?

If you couldn't answer all three clearly... you have a problem.

### The 5-Second Rule

Here's a stat that should terrify every business owner: **you have about 5 seconds before a website visitor decides to stay or leave.**

Five seconds.

That's not enough time to read your mission statement. Or scroll through your services. Or admire your portfolio.

It's barely enough time to form an impression.

And if that impression is "I don't know what this is or what I'm supposed to do"‚Äîthey're gone. Forever.

### The "Grunt Test"

Donald Miller calls this the "Grunt Test." Could a caveman look at your website, grunt, and understand:

1. What you offer
2. How it makes their life better
3. What to do next

Most websites fail this test spectacularly.

### The Anatomy of a High-Converting Homepage

Let me break down exactly what needs to be above the fold (visible without scrolling) on your homepage:

**1. The Header (Most Important)**

This is your one-liner. It needs to communicate the transformation you provide.

‚ùå Bad: "Welcome to ABC Company"
‚ùå Bad: "Quality Service Since 1985"
‚ùå Bad: "Your Trusted Partner"

‚úÖ Good: "Get Your Weekends Back With Professional Lawn Care"
‚úÖ Good: "Turn Website Visitors Into Paying Customers"
‚úÖ Good: "Sell Your Land Fast for a Fair Cash Offer"

**The Formula:** [Desired Outcome] + [Timeframe or Method] + [Without Pain/With Benefit]

**2. The Subheader**

Clarifies who it's for or how you do it.

Example: "Reliable, affordable lawn care for busy homeowners in Columbus."

**3. The Call to Action (CTA)**

ONE clear button. Not three. Not "Learn More." A direct action.

- "Get Your Free Quote"
- "Schedule a Call"
- "Start Your Free Trial"

Make the button color stand out. Make the text action-oriented.

**4. The Image**

Show the end result. Happy customers. The transformation. Not your logo, not a generic stock photo, not your building.

Show people what success looks like.

### Below the Fold

Once they scroll, here's what should come next:

**The Problem Section**
Acknowledge their pain. "You work hard all week. The last thing you want is to spend Saturday fighting with your lawn."

**The Stakes**
What happens if they don't solve this? "Meanwhile, your property value drops and neighbors start to talk."

**Your 3-Step Plan**
Simple. Visual. Numbered.
1. "Tell us about your property"
2. "Get a custom quote in 24 hours"
3. "Enjoy a lawn you're proud of"

**Social Proof**
Testimonials. Logos of clients. Star ratings. Results.

**Another CTA**
Repeat your call to action. They should never have to scroll up to take action.

# EMAIL 4: The Authority Play
## "How to Become the Obvious Choice in Your Market"

**Subject Line:** The strategy that makes selling easy
**Send:** Day 7

---

Hey {{first_name}},

Let me tell you about two plumbers.

**Plumber A** runs some Facebook ads, has a basic website, and waits for the phone to ring. When someone calls, they're shopping around, comparing prices. Every job is a negotiation.

**Plumber B** posts helpful videos on YouTube‚Äî"How to unclog a drain without chemicals," "5 signs your water heater is about to fail," "What to look for when buying an older home." He writes a monthly email newsletter with seasonal plumbing tips. Local homeowners follow him. Real estate agents recommend him.

When Plumber B's phone rings, the conversation is different. "Hey, I've been watching your videos. I have an issue‚Äîcan you come take a look?"

No price shopping. No comparing quotes. They already trust him.

**This is the difference between chasing customers and attracting them.**

### The Authority Hierarchy

Building authority isn't complicated, but it takes consistency. Here's the hierarchy of content, from foundational to advanced:

**Level 1: Social Media (Awareness)**
- Where: LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok‚Äîpick 1-2 max
- What: Quick tips, behind-the-scenes, client wins, your perspective
- Why: Builds familiarity over time
- Effort: 3-5 posts per week

**Level 2: Long-Form Content (Discovery)**
- Where: Blog, YouTube, podcast
- What: In-depth answers to questions your customers are asking
- Why: Gets found through search (Google, YouTube)
- Effort: 1-2 pieces per week

**Level 3: Email Newsletter (Nurture)**
- Where: Their inbox
- What: Valuable insights, stories, occasional offers
- Why: Builds relationship, stays top of mind
- Effort: Weekly or bi-weekly

**Level 4: Lead Magnets (Capture)**
- Where: Your website, social bios, ads
- What: Free guides, checklists, templates, assessments
- Why: Exchanges value for contact info
- Effort: Create 1-3 solid ones, promote ongoing

**Level 5: Borrowed Platforms (Credibility)**
- Where: Podcasts, guest posts, PR, speaking
- What: Your expertise on other people's stages
- Why: Instant credibility boost
- Effort: Outreach-based, as opportunities arise

### The "What Should I Post?" Problem

The #1 reason business owners don't create content: they don't know what to say.

Here's a simple fix. **Answer the questions you already get asked.**

Think about the last 20 conversations you had with customers or prospects. What did they ask?

- "How much does this cost?"
- "How long does it take?"
- "What's the difference between X and Y?"
- "Is this worth it?"
- "What should I look for when hiring someone like you?"

Each of those questions is a piece of content:
- Blog post: "How Much Does [Your Service] Really Cost in 2024?"
- Video: "X vs Y: Which Is Right for You?"
- Social post: "3 red flags when hiring a [your industry]"

**You already have the content. You just haven't written it down yet.**

### The One-Platform Strategy

Here's a mistake I see constantly: trying to be everywhere at once.

Don't do this. Pick ONE platform and master it before expanding.

**If you're B2B or professional services:** Start with LinkedIn
**If you're local/service-based:** Start with Google Business Profile + Facebook
**If you're visual (before/after, products):** Start with Instagram
**If you can teach on camera:** Start with YouTube
**If your audience is younger:** Consider TikTok

The algorithm rewards consistency. Posting three times a week on one platform beats posting once a week on five platforms.

### The Compound Effect

Here's what most people miss: content compounds.

That blog post you write today? It might get 10 views this month. But if it ranks on Google, it could get 10 views every day for years.

That YouTube video? Might get 50 views in the first week. But YouTube keeps recommending evergreen content. Three years later, it's been watched 50,000 times.

Every piece of content you create is a little salesperson working 24/7.

### Your Action Step

This week:

1. **Choose your platform.** One. Just one.
2. **List your top 10 FAQs.** Questions customers actually ask.
3. **Create your first piece.** Answer one FAQ in detail.
4. **Post it.** Don't overthink it.
5. **Repeat.** Same time, same days, every week.

Here's a simple content calendar to start:

**Monday:** Answer a common question
**Wednesday:** Share a client win or behind-the-scenes
**Friday:** Give your opinion on something in your industry

That's it. Three posts a week. Consistent. Valuable.

### The Long Game

I won't lie to you: content marketing takes time. You won't see results in a week or even a month.

But six months from now, you'll have 75+ pieces of content working for you. A year from now, you'll be the name that comes up when people ask for recommendations.

The best time to start was a year ago. The second best time is today.

# EMAIL 5: The Pipeline Builder
## "How to Capture Leads Before They're Ready to Buy"

**Subject Line:** The follow-up fortune most businesses miss
**Send:** Day 10

---

Hey {{first_name}},

Here's a marketing truth that changed how I think about sales:

**Most people who visit your website aren't ready to buy today.**

Maybe they're researching. Maybe they're comparing options. Maybe they're not even sure they have a problem worth solving yet.

If your only call-to-action is "Contact Us" or "Get a Quote," you're only capturing the tiny percentage who are ready right now.

Everyone else? They leave your site, forget about you, and when they ARE ready... they can't remember your name.

### The Lead Magnet Solution

A lead magnet is something valuable you give away for free in exchange for an email address. It's sometimes called an "ethical bribe."

The psychology is simple: humans are wired for reciprocity. When you give them something valuable, they feel obligated to pay attention to you.

But more importantly, **you now have permission to follow up.**

That person who wasn't ready to buy today? Now you can email them weekly. You can nurture the relationship. And when they ARE ready‚Äîguess who they think of first?

### What Makes a Great Lead Magnet?

Not all lead magnets are created equal. Here's what separates the ones that work from the ones that collect dust:

**1. Solves a Specific Problem**
Not "Everything You Need to Know About X." Instead: "The 5-Minute Fix for [Specific Problem]."

Specific beats comprehensive. People want quick wins.

**2. Provides Immediate Value**
They should get value in minutes, not hours. If your lead magnet requires a 2-hour time investment, they'll download it and never open it.

**3. Naturally Leads to Your Service**
The best lead magnets create awareness of a bigger problem‚Äîone you can solve.

Example: "The 10-Point Home Maintenance Checklist" ‚Üí Helps homeowners ‚Üí Makes them realize their HVAC needs servicing ‚Üí Guess who they call?

### Lead Magnet Ideas by Industry

**Service Businesses:**
- "The 5-Point Checklist Before Hiring a [Your Service]"
- "10 Questions to Ask Any [Your Industry] Before Signing"
- "The [Your City] Homeowner's Seasonal Maintenance Guide"

**Consultants & Agencies:**
- "Free Assessment: Find Your Biggest [Problem] in 5 Minutes"
- "The [Industry] Playbook for 2024"
- "Case Study: How [Client] Achieved [Result]"

**B2B Companies:**
- "ROI Calculator: Is [Your Solution] Worth It?"
- "The [Industry] Benchmark Report"
- "Template: [Something They Need to Do Regularly]"

**Local Businesses:**
- "The Insider's Guide to [Your City/Service]"
- "First-Timer's Checklist for [Your Service]"
- "$50 Off Your First [Service]" (simple but effective)

### The Mechanics: How to Create One

**Step 1: Choose Your Format**
- **PDF/Checklist:** Easiest to create (Canva makes this simple)
- **Quiz/Assessment:** Higher engagement, more complex (try Typeform or ScoreApp)
- **Video Training:** More personal, builds connection (Loom is free)
- **Template:** High value if your audience needs to do something regularly

**Step 2: Create the Content**
Take that FAQ list from the last email. Pick the most common question. Answer it thoroughly but concisely. That's your lead magnet.

Example: If people always ask "How do I know if I need a new roof?", create "The 7 Warning Signs You Need a New Roof (and 3 Signs You Don't)."

**Step 3: Design It**
Canva has templates for everything. It doesn't need to be fancy‚Äîit needs to be useful and readable.

Include:
- Your logo
- Clear headlines
- Actionable content
- A call-to-action at the end (soft pitch for your services)

**Step 4: Set Up the Delivery**
You need:
- A landing page with an opt-in form
- An email service to deliver it automatically
- A follow-up sequence (even just a "Here's your download!" email)

Tools: ConvertKit, Mailchimp, and GoHighLevel all make this easy.

### The Follow-Up Sequence

Here's what happens after someone downloads your lead magnet:

**Email 1 (Immediate):** Deliver the download + welcome them
**Email 2 (Day 2):** Ask if they had any questions + provide extra value
**Email 3 (Day 4):** Share a relevant success story
**Email 4 (Day 7):** Introduce your services (soft pitch)
**Ongoing:** Add them to your weekly newsletter

The goal isn't to sell immediately. It's to stay top of mind until they're ready.

### Your Action Step

This week, create a simple lead magnet:

1. **Pick your top FAQ** (the question you answer most often)
2. **Write out your answer** (aim for 1-3 pages, not a novel)
3. **Design it in Canva** (search "lead magnet template")
4. **Put it on your website** (even a simple pop-up works)
5. **Promote it** (mention it in social posts, email signature)

Don't overcomplicate this. A simple, useful checklist beats an elaborate ebook that never gets finished.

# EMAIL 6: The Paid Amplifier
## "How to Scale With Advertising (Without Burning Money)"

**Subject Line:** The truth about paid ads (that agencies won't tell you)
**Send:** Day 14

---

Hey {{first_name}},

Let's talk about the elephant in the room: paid advertising.

You've probably heard the horror stories. Business owners who spent thousands on Facebook ads and got nothing. Google Ads accounts that drained budgets overnight. Promises of "guaranteed leads" that never materialized.

I'm going to tell you the truth about paid ads‚Äîincluding when you should NOT run them.

### The Pre-Requisites

Paid advertising is like pouring gasoline on a fire. If you have a fire (a working funnel), gasoline makes it bigger. If you don't have a fire (broken messaging, bad website), gasoline just makes a mess.

**Before you spend a dollar on ads, you need:**

‚úÖ Clear messaging (who you help, how, and why it matters)
‚úÖ A website that converts (passes the 5-second test)
‚úÖ A lead magnet or clear offer
‚úÖ A follow-up system (at minimum, an email sequence)
‚úÖ Tracking in place (you need to know what's working)

If you're missing any of these, fix them first. Ads won't save a broken foundation‚Äîthey'll just expose it faster.

### The Paid Media Hierarchy

Not all ad platforms are equal. Here's how to think about them:

**Tier 1: Search Ads (Google, Bing) ‚Äî Capture Existing Demand**
- Best for: Services people actively search for
- Example: "plumber near me," "best CRM for small business," "divorce attorney Austin"
- You're capturing people who already want what you sell
- Generally highest intent, highest conversion rates

**Tier 2: Social Ads (Meta, LinkedIn, TikTok) ‚Äî Create New Demand**
- Best for: Services people don't know they need, or brand building
- Example: Targeting small business owners with a free guide on brand strategy
- You're interrupting their scroll, so you need a compelling hook
- Lower intent, but massive reach

**Tier 3: Retargeting ‚Äî Stay Top of Mind**
- Best for: People who visited your site but didn't convert
- Example: "Still thinking about it? Here's a case study..."
- Warmest audience you can advertise to
- Usually lowest cost per conversion

**Tier 4: Display, YouTube, Streaming, Podcasts ‚Äî Brand Building**
- Best for: Broader awareness and credibility
- Example: Sponsoring a podcast your audience listens to
- Longer-term play, harder to measure directly
- Best when combined with direct response

### The Golden Rule

Here's the rule that will save you thousands:

**Never send cold ad traffic directly to a sales page.**

Cold traffic = people who've never heard of you. They're not ready to buy. If you ask for the sale immediately, you'll get terrible results and blame "ads don't work."

Instead, use this funnel:

```
Ad ‚Üí Lead Magnet Landing Page ‚Üí Email Nurture ‚Üí Sales Conversation
```

The ad's job isn't to sell. It's to get someone to raise their hand and say, "Yes, I'm interested in this topic."

Then your email sequence does the heavy lifting of building trust and demonstrating expertise.

### Starting Your First Campaign

If you've got your foundation in place and want to test paid ads, here's how to start:

**Budget:** $500-$1,000/month to test (less than this and you won't have enough data)

**Platform Selection:**
- If people search for your service ‚Üí Start with Google Ads
- If they don't search (or you're building awareness) ‚Üí Start with Meta (Facebook/Instagram)

**The Setup:**
1. Install tracking pixels on your website (Google Analytics, Meta Pixel)
2. Create a dedicated landing page for your ad (not your homepage)
3. Write 3-5 ad variations to test
4. Define your target audience
5. Set a daily budget (start with $20-50/day)
6. Let it run for at least 2 weeks before judging

**What to Track:**
- Cost per click (CPC)
- Landing page conversion rate
- Cost per lead (CPL)
- Lead to customer conversion rate
- Customer acquisition cost (CAC)

### The Honest Truth

Here's what most agencies won't tell you:

**Paid ads are not "set it and forget it."** They require constant monitoring, testing, and optimization.

**The first campaigns usually lose money.** You're buying data about what works. Expect the first month or two to be education, not profit.

**Platform changes break things.** iOS updates, algorithm changes, policy updates‚Äîsomething is always shifting.

**The best advertisers are the best analysts.** Success comes from obsessively watching the numbers and making incremental improvements.

This is why many small businesses are better off waiting until they have the budget to either:
- Dedicate significant time to learning paid ads themselves
- Hire someone (internal or agency) who lives in this world

### When to NOT Run Ads

Don't run paid ads if:
- Your website doesn't convert organic traffic
- You can't track leads and conversions
- You don't have $1,000+ to test over 2-3 months
